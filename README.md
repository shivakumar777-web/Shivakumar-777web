\section{Introduction}
In classical physics, time and space are treated as symmetric dimensions (apart from the metric signature), but our subjective experience of time is inherently directional. VSDM begins by noting that Vedic cosmology naturally partitions the lunar month into 30 tithis and the sidereal zodiac into 27 nak·π£atras. This implies a numeric offset of 3 between the basic time unit count (30) and spatial sector count (27). We propose that $\Delta=3$ is not a coincidence but a fundamental asymmetry---a ``cosmic offset''---that seeds the arrow of time.

The significance of this offset can be understood by examining the traditional Vedic astronomical system. In this system, the lunar month is meticulously divided into 30 tithis, each representing a specific phase of the moon's relationship with the sun. Concurrently, the ecliptic is segmented into 27 nak·π£atras, which are not only constellations but also markers of time in the lunar calendar. The difference between these two fundamental units---30 and 27---yields an offset of 3, which we argue is not arbitrary but a reflection of a deeper cosmological principle. This principle suggests that time and space, while interconnected, are not symmetric, with time possessing a directional quality that space does not inherently have.

This resonates with recent physics ideas: for example, loop quantum gravity (LQG) posits that space-time is built from discrete quanta and that time may emerge from quantum interactions. \section{Model Formulation}
We now formulate VSDM in detail. Let an integer $t \in \{0,1,\dots,29\}$ index the current tithi (lunar day) and $s \in \{0,1,\dots,26\}$ index the current nak·π£atra (lunar mansion). Classically, one would expect $s$ and $t$ to be independent cycling variables. Instead, we impose a fixed relation
\[
s \equiv t + \Delta \pmod{27}, \qquad \Delta = 3.
\]
This relation can be visualized as two clocks: one ticking 30 times per lunar month (the tithi clock) and the other ticking 27 times per sidereal year (the nak·π£atra clock). The offset $\Delta=3$ means that the tithi clock is always ahead of the nak·π£atra clock by 3 units, creating a perpetual phase difference. In physical terms, this can be analogized to a system where time and space are out of sync by a fixed amount, leading to an inherent directionality in time.

The phase difference can be expressed as:
\[
\phi_t = \frac{2\pi t}{30}, \quad \psi_s = \frac{2\pi s}{27}, \quad 
\psi_{t+\Delta} - \phi_t = \frac{2\pi}{9}.
\]
To embed this in a physical picture, consider a periodic field $\Phi(t,s)$ defined on the discrete torus $\mathbb{Z}_{30} \times \mathbb{Z}_{27}$. The offset is implemented by the constraint $\Phi(t,s)=0$ unless $s \equiv t+\Delta$. In a continuous analog, one might introduce angular coordinates $\theta \in [0,2\pi)$ for time and $\phi \in [0,2\pi)$ for space with incommensurate periods, so that $\theta$ ``runs ahead'' by a constant phase:
\[
\theta = \frac{2\pi t}{30}, \quad \phi = \frac{2\pi (t+\Delta)}{27}, \quad \theta - \phi = -\frac{2\pi\Delta}{27} = -\frac{2\pi}{9}.
\]
This nonzero phase difference prevents a symmetric treatment of $\theta$ and $\phi$. In fact, one can derive effective equations of motion that explicitly break time-reversal symmetry. For example, a toy model Hamiltonian might be written as 
\[
H = H_0(\theta,\phi) + \epsilon \cos(3\,\theta - 3\,\phi),
\]
where the coupling term enforces the asymmetry. Importantly, this discrete $\Delta$ offset is analogous to mechanisms in quantum theories of time. As Coppo et al. discuss in Physical Review A (2024), the Page--Wootters scheme makes time emerge only through entanglement with a clock \cite{coppo2024}. VSDM similarly makes ``clock-time'' contingent on a deep relational structure (the Vedic cycles).
